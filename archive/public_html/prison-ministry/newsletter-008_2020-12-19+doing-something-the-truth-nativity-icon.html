A Word from Fr David

A “little something” is better than a “whole lot of nothing.”

Dear brothers and sisters, I pray this letter finds you well! In many letters, I hear about the hardships and altered schedules you are dealing with at the present time due to COVID lockdowns and limitations. This situation can lead to the temptation to cave-in and surrender to circumstance rather than creatively and resolutely defend your "non-negotiables," such as Morning and Evening Prayers, or keeping the Nativity Fast. May the Lord grant us all good strength and courage for our daily struggle to be an Orthodox Christian!

"The Lord cares for the salvation of your soul more than you think. He will save you if you just turn to Him with humility and hope and do even what seems to be little. The Lord God greatly values even the little, if it is done for His sake." - St. Moses of Optina

It is always better to do "a little something" than "a whole lotta nothing": offering even one "Our Father" from a broken heart before sleep is infinitely better than ignoring our all-forgiving God because we're exhausted by sin and ashamed of ourselves. But for those days or hours when we slack off and do nothing, are we then permitted by God to retreat into a corner and spend the next day or week in the mire of despondency and carelessness because "I messed up"? No, dear ones, we slaves of Christ don't have the right to; the Lord has not blessed us to do so! You won't find the following commandment in the Gospel: "If you sin once, keep sinning; for what is the point of keeping God's commandments if you can't keep them perfectly?" (That's actually one of the evil one's preferred lies, one that he whispers to us after our falls into sin. Don't believe it for a moment.) Heed these words instead:

"If you happen to sin- believe in the mercy of God; offer up repentance and keep going without being disturbed." - St. Barsanuphius of Optina

Our life in Christ consists in the following: falling and rising, falling and rising again... begging the Lord's forgiveness (especially through Psalm 50 and the Jesus Prayer), entreating His help, and glorifying Him for every new and undeserved beginning. That we sin each day is no surprise; that God forgives and renews the contrite soul is a wondrous surprise every time! The more you lay aside your will - "What do I feel like doing today?" - and follow after God's - "What does God ask of me today?" - you will discover: God's will for us is good and leads to our salvation.

My family and I are well, by God's grace. We all have COVID, but our symptoms are mild and rapidly improving. We are quarantined at home until Sunday, December 20th. Who knows where we picked it up... it's everywhere right now. Many of our parishioners at St. Jonah's have it, too, but thank God, no one is seriously ill and all seem to be making good recoveries. For us and many others, it manifests as a mild cold and clears up within a few days. We have been entreating St. Nikiforos the Leper for his prayers since the beginning of this pandemic, and we are sure that his care is evident in this present trial. He is a 20th-century monastic saint who endured a grievous lifelong illness and was known for comforting, encouraging, and spiritually instructing others around him, while maintaining bright joy and gratitude to God in the midst of his own suffering. He appeared in visions to a few Orthodox Christians at the beginning of this year, promising his intercessions before Christ for all who call upon him for deliverance from the pandemic. St. Nikiforos, pray to God for us!

"The devil plows, but Christ sows the seeds." - St. Paisios of Mount Athos. I've been reflecting on this word of the saint recently. No matter what sins, temptations, and sorrows we may experience, every furrow cut through our heart, or even through our Society, can be a place where Christ plants the good seeds: here can spring up a new awareness of our tragic condition, repentance, humility, longing for the Lord, mercy toward our neighbor who is also suffering... it is never too late for God to save us, or to bring good out of our evil circumstances! Only, we must repent, beg His help, and glorify His all-good Providence.

We've been doing a little work around our yard to plant some "seeds" this year... fruit trees, vegetable gardens, and now chickens! We have two, purchased from a feed store near the beginning of November. They've begun to lay eggs this past week, and we've collected about 10 so far. The kids love these birds- and I'm rather fond of them, too. The chickens are basically pets, roaming the backyard and running to see us when we come outside. We can't eat eggs at the moment, of course, so we're saving the eggs to give to someone who doesn't fast. That's the news around these parts...

I'll close for now, with prayer for your health and salvation in body and soul. Pray for us. With love in the Lord,Fr. David

 

Life of New Martyr Anna of Kurd

December 22 \ January 4 2013.There are martyrs in our time.

Anna M.Kaloyan is one of New Martyrs who suffered for Christ in contemporary post-Soviet time. Anna Kaloyan was born on December 18, 1987 in a Kurdish Yezidi family and lived in Bazkovskaya village of Sholokhov district in Rostov region. In autumn 2012 the girl accepted Christ and converted to Christianity.

Valery Kharitonov, the senior priest of Sretensky monastery, administered theSacrament of baptism. Anna became an active parishioner notwithstanding threats from her Yezidi relatives and tried to spend all her free time in the church. She was helpful and eager to do any chores. The girl’s parents, her relatives and all Yezidi community were trying to persuade Anna to deny Christ and to return to Yezidi religion, but all their attempts to force her to do it, which included beatings and threatening, were in vain.

With the beginning of the Nativity fast Anna often confessed and took Holy communion. She was preparing to celebrate Christmas and sewed herself a beautiful dress, but she was not destined to experience the holiday joy on earth. In the early hours of January 4, 2013 the girl was harried to death by her own parents. At first they broke Anna’s legs so that she couldn’t run away, then her father started to beat her entire body with steel rods while mother was hitting her head with a wood log. Each blow was accompanied with the demand to deny Christ, but each time all the way to her death the martyr firmly refused to do it.

Thus, on the day when Holy Church celebrates the feast of Anastasia of Sermium, Anna Kaloyan sufferd martyrdom for the love of Christ at the hands of her own parents. The martyr’s father pledged himself guilty for the murder of his daughter and was sentenced to 7 years in prison. The martyr was buried near village Verkhnetokinsky in Yezidi cemetery away from Yezidi graves. The martyr’s grave is honored by Orthodox Christians.

I was able to speak to Vasiliy Khadykin, the archpriest of Shakhtinskaya Episcopate, and he reported thatat Verkhny Don Anna Kaloyan is honored as a Holy Martyr. As the head of Episcopate committee for Saints canonization he is gathering material for glorifying the New Martyr in Russian Orthodox Church.

Monk Madai (Maamdi)  Source: «Five years of ministry» -Shakhtinskaya Episcopate, p. 92

Questions & Answers

All answers by Fr Seraphim

Who is the patron of your church?

The patron saint of our church is St. Nicholas, Bishop of Myra and Lycia, the Wonderworker. He is the same St. Nicholas that some people have referred to as Santa Claus. Of course, Santa Claus is an invention of Western commerce. But there is a Saint Nicholas, and he is the patron of our church. St. Jonah of Manchuria was a bishop in Russia. He was quite holy and died relatively recently, in the past century. He is the patron saint of the church that father David serves that.

Who is the patron of the Prison Ministry?

 I consider the patron of our prison ministry to be St. Joseph the all comely. That is Patriarch Joseph, the second youngest son of Jacob. He was sold off to Egypt and went to prison and then, later on, saved all of his brethren from famine and was reunited with them.

He is considered to be a type of Christ. A type is something that points to a promised reality. For instance, passing through the Red Sea is a type of baptism; it points to baptism. Moses striking the water with a rod points to the cross because, of course, the rod is wooden. The Lamb that was sacrificed on Passover is a type of Christ. There are many human beings in the Old Testament who are types of Christ such as Moses, Joseph the all comely, Jonah the prophet. Those are a few. Another type that is very well known is circumcision. It is something that points to baptism. Since we have baptism now, we are no longer required to circumcise our male children. Also, since we have the Holy Eucharist, we no longer sacrifice a lamb.

Who is my Spiritual Father?

I am your spiritual father. It appears that I will be the one that baptizes you and catechizes you (of course, if you do not know me but father David has been catechizing you, then he course is your spiritual father) I pray for you every day, and therefore you should pray for me every day. This is a relationship, and it does not necessarily need proximity. Prayer shortens the distance between us.

When not in a monastery, the spiritual father functions a little differently. We should have more discussions about this if you have questions.

“For though ye have ten thousand instructors in Christ, yet have ye not many fathers: for in Christ Jesus I have begotten you through the gospel.” 1Cor 4:15 

 

When do I get a guardian angel, and what is his name?

We never name our Guardian Angel. However, every Christian has a guardian angel. There are some who think that there is an angel that protects every single person who was born, even before their baptism. There is a prayer in the making of the catechumen service, which I will serve when I see you, where a petition is made that says “yoke unto his or her life a Guardian Angel”. There are also those who believe the Guardian Angel begins to protect us upon our baptism. I think you should just start praying to your Guardian Angel. God knows who he will be and your Angel probably knows also. Whether or not your Guardian Angel is actually protecting you right now, absolutely, positively, the angels are protecting you. Without the angels protecting us, Satan would burn the earth.

Your guardian angel is not based upon your birthdate or date of baptism. God chooses who is to take care of you. Your name at baptism can be many things. I advise anyone to use the birth-name they had been given, if it is the name of the saint. However sometimes someone gets very attached to a particular saint, and it is permissible to change your name. You should ask me about this though, don’t just come Up with the name. It has been a tradition where people will name their child based upon the birthdate. This is not an absolute.

How will I be received into the church?

When I receive you into the Orthodox Church, I will baptize you. We consider that any baptism outside of the Orthodox Church is only the form of baptism. I’m glad if anyone makes any kind of commitment to Christ outside the church, such as baptism in jail by the chaplain or some volunteer, but we don’t consider that to be the baptism of the church. I’m not saying anything negative about it, nor am I criticizing, but only the church is capable of baptizing.

It is our practice always to baptize people coming to the church, so there is no ambiguity. There are other practices, but we don’t do them because there’s too much chance of a person saying something like, “I was already baptized, and so they couldn’t baptize me twice; that’s why I was chrismated.” Well, if the person is already baptized, they are already in the church! What are you doing, coming to the Orthodox Church if you are already in the church? Of course, a person is not in the church until they are in the church, baptized, or in some rare cases, received in another way into the Orthodox Church. The entry into the church is baptism. There are other cases where a person can be chrismated, but I don’t do it because I don’t want any confusion. If this is confusing to you, I will explain it to you in person. Of course, you may ask any question that you want to ask.

How should I think about Roman Catholic practices of piety, such as the adoration of the Blessed sacrament?

We should be simple people. To be simple as not to be stupid or uninformed. It is to be humble. Simple people love the church and measure all truth against the church. Since the church is the body of Christ, we measure all truth against Christ. The simple answer to this question is that if the church does not have a practice, it should not be done.

The longer answer is that “the adoration of the Blessed Sacrament” is a shallow reflection of our Jesus prayer. It is the practice of coming to the Roman Catholic temple and usually sitting or kneeling in the presence of the reserved sacrament, which may be open to display or just indicated with the candle. Of course, a person would usually be praying during this time. Sometimes the priest leads formal prayers. Orthodox Christians know nothing of this practice because we say the prayer of Jesus or any prayers wherever we are, and we do think a place makes the prayer more powerful.

Every Orthodox Church has the reserved sacrament, usually on the altar. Any priest may save the reserved sacrament by celebrating the liturgy, and taking the lamb which has been consecrated, that is, become the body of Christ, and dipping it into the blood in the chalice or pouring some of the blood onto it from a spoon, and allowing it to dry. After it dries, it is cut into small pieces. We use reserved sacrament to commune the sick or to bring the sacrament to people when we cannot celebrate the liturgy, such as in prison.

The name of Jesus is mighty, not because we say it with our lips, but because we live according to who Jesus Christ is in our hearts. The Jesus prayer is in some way similar to the adoration of the Blessed Sacrament. There is an emphasis on Jesus Christ; however, for the Latins, the focus is physical and corporeal and depends on a specific place. We do not consider any particular place to be necessary to say the Jesus prayer.

I don’t have time to go into the history of the adoration of the Blessed Sacrament. It is enough for me to say the Jesus prayer and to reject any practice that is foreign to the Orthodox Church.  Be aware of FOMO (fear of missing out). The Orthodox Church is the body of Christ, and therefore nothing is lacking in her. Trying to find practices which you formerly had in your previous belief that correspond to Orthodoxy is fraught with peril.  It is asking the wrong questions.

A Word from Fr Seraphim

Are you standing for the truth, or is the truth standing in you?

 

Many people look at the lies and schemes in our Society, and they are  Facebook or Twitter "Warriors" on the "side of the truth." Even those who do not use social media can fancy themselves “warriors” on the side of the truth. We must be aware of the lies and delusions, and depravities of our Society's political and cultural, and educational systems.  It is critical to know the truth and live according to it, but for most people, to “stand on the side of the truth” mostly means obsessively expressing their opinions. This is not being in the truth as much as being in a state of perpetual anger.

Christians do not “stand on the side of the truth.” The truth is Jesus Christ. Christians have the truth living inside them.  Living according to the truth is not primarily expressing our opinions. Living according to the truth is to become like the truth that lives within us, that is, to become like Jesus Christ.

Sure, have opinions about abortion and about the election, and about covid-19, about the vaccine, and censorship, and about this politician and that politician. But none of that stuff is going to save you. Certainly, being angry about that stuff is not going to save you. A compulsion to express your opinions about that stuff is not going to save you.

And what are you going to do when you find the truth is not supported? It rarely is. Why are you surprised? How many memes can you make to stand by the truth, or opinions can you express in the day room when the truth should be living inside of you?

If you're not praying, and prostrating, and saying the Jesus prayer, or saying an Akathist every day, or doing all of it, then what you are doing on Facebook and Twitter, or at the water cooler, or in the day room, or the office is meaningless for your soul.

We are praying the Akathist most evenings in the church, and some of us are praying it every evening, whether in the church or at home. I invite you to join us in this; it will help Society have more truth than a thousand Facebook memes or a thousand of our opinions.

Society is continuously full of hoaxes, and dirty secrets, and depravity that is promoted and lauded. There are lies and delusions about everything. The greatest illusion is that people live for now and identify with whatever they want. They equate politics with life and social slogans as Gospel.  Do not make the mistake of thinking that you live for the truth if you recognize lies in Society! This is just another false identity.

We will not be saved by a meme! We will not be saved by “standing” for the truth! We will be saved if we live an interior life.


The tsunami is coming. No amount of Facebook posts will stop it. Are you ready for it?

If you are not praying the Jesus prayer and doing prostrations and keeping the fast and struggling for virtue every day, then you're not ready. If you are able to come to church services, but you do not, you are not ready.

You are a priest! Do you understand this? All of us are priests. We are all of the royal priesthood, and we are all called to intercede for the world! Posting memes on Facebook or having opinions is not interceding for the world. If having opinions is our idea of interceding for the world, and if this is what we do, we are not Redeeming the time.

The person who is able must come to church and pray. Even if there are two or three people present, your prayer is very POWERFUL.

What should a person do if they cannot come to the church? You, my brothers and sisters in prison, have a pretty good reason why you cannot come to services. Most people in the so-called free world do not have a good reason, especially in our parish, which has services every single day. Every Christian must daily stand and pray in intercession for everyone who crosses their path, all those who trouble them, and the entire world. When you do this, your prayer is more powerful than a thousand viral Facebook posts or a thousand opinionated comments. It is priestly prayer.

Don't live the spiritual life in your head and with your fingers on the keyboard, or with your loud and opinionated mouth! Live it with your fists on the ground when you do prostrations, and with your fingers making the sign of the cross multiple times, and with your body standing in prayer during the vigil and the liturgy

A Primer for the Christian Beginning to Lead a Spiritual Life – 15-20

From "A Primer for the Christian Beginning to Lead a Spiritual Life", Hieroconfessor Alexander (Orlov) Of Omsk, who fell asleep in 1977 at the age of 99, translated by Protopresbyter Thomas Marretta. These gems of spiritual wisdom are sent in email messages from my good friend, Fr Demetrios Carellas. We will investigate getting copies of this book to send to many of you. The comments (in italics) are from Fr Dimitri Carellas (Priest Seraphim)

 

15.The man determined to lead a spiritual life must understand that he is sick, that his thoughts are in error, that his will is more inclined to evil than to good, and that his heart is defiled by boiling, seething passions. The goal of the first stage of spiritual life is to restore a measure of health to the soul.

16. Spiritual life is an unending, never-abating war with the enemies of our salvation. Never allow your soul to fall asleep!.  Your spirit should always be alert.  In this battle you must never tire of calling upon your Savior for help

17.Be watchful, lest you consent to the sinful thoughts assailing you.  Never allow conjunction between them and your mind.  If you give assent to such thoughts, you have already sinned (or as St. Paisios the Athonite teaches us: Do not “build runways for them to land in your heart”).

18. If you are heedless, then you will certainly perish.

19. Ask the Lord to grant you fear of God.  Oh, how blessed are they who ever tremble before the Lord! ​

20. Give God your whole heart, without reservation, and you will experience Paradise on earth.



Explanation of the icon of the Nativity of the Savior.

You may not receive another newsletter after this one until after Nativity, which we celebrate on January 7. Of course, most people in prison will be celebrating Christmas on December 25, and perhaps the description of this icon may be useful to you for that time.

Please read the endnotes when you encounter them.

They are part of this instructive text and were written to be read as part of the text.

All of our icons teach precious theological truths. There is a long-standing tradition of including particular items in icons to teach dogmatic truths. The icons of the great feasts are particularly rich in these images.

The Theotokos[1] is in the center of the Icon of the Nativity and is repose with our Lord Jesus Christ wrapped in swaddling clothes in the manger with the ox and the donkey.

Our Lord Jesus Christ is shown looking like a young boy, not an infant, because he is God and man, and even as an infant, He was not helpless. He is also lying in a crib that looks similar to a casket and wrapped in swaddling clothes, which also looks like a burial shroud. These elements presage his eventual death on the cross and wrapped in a burial shroud by Joseph and Nicodemus.[2]

The Theotokos is shown in repose and lying on a red blanket. Red is the color of blood and life. She is shown in repose because we know from Holy Tradition that her birth-giving was without travail[3]. Our dogma states that the birth of Jesus Christ was miraculous, just as his conception was. The Mother of God remained a virgin during birth and after birth, just as before birth. She did not escape the pain of motherhood, however, because it was prophesied that when her Son was crucified, a sword would pass through her heart.[4]

The ox and the donkey reference to the prophecy from Isaiah 1:3: “The ox knows his owner and the ass knows his master’s crib. But Israel does not know, and my people do not consider me.”

 The star points to the manger, as the Gospel of Matthew describes it.[5] This “star” was an angelic power, and some say it was the Archangel Gabriel. It could not be anything other than an angel because it displayed intelligence and behavior totally unlike a star. This star appeared in the East and traveled from East to west and from north to south, which is unlike how a star travels in that area of the world. It also was visible by day. Everyone knows that stars disappear when the sun comes up. It even vanished at will when the Magi came to Jerusalem.[6] They were the only ones that could see the star, and when it disappeared, they were troubled, and they went to Herod and Herod consulted his wise men, and they remembered the prophecy that said that the Savior would be born in Bethlehem[7].

 Since Herod could not see the star, he had no way of finding the Christ child, so he resorted to subterfuge with the Magi and gave them leave to worship the Christ child and asked them that they would return to report the child’s location so that he could also worship the child.[8] Of course, Herod had no desire to worship the Christ child but instead planned to kill him, and later the Magi were told of Herod’s plans. They came back to their country another way[9] , and Herod was left to fume with anger. After this, Herod decided to kill all of the infants in the area from two years old and younger, in a futile effort to kill the Christ child.[10]

It is unknown exactly when the Magi appeared to worship the Christ child. Matthew says that they were in a house, and Christ was certainly born in a manger[11], a cave in the hills used to shelter animals. The Magi probably appeared shortly after Christ’s birth because, after his 40th day, Joseph was warned in a dream, and they likely left for Egypt to escape Herod.[12] In any case, the family certainly left for Egypt during Jesus' early infancy.

The Magi are shown in this icon in the center to the left. They were Persian astrologers looking for a sign in the sky, according to their ancient teacher Balaam[13]. When this sign appeared, they journeyed to Jerusalem following the angel. Tradition names these three Magi as Caspar, Balthasar, and Melchor. They brought three gifts according to the Evangelist Matthew: gold, frankincense, and myrrh.  

Gold is appropriate to give to a king because a king is rich. The King of the entire universe was lying like a peasant in a manger, but nonetheless, He is a king, and in the next coming, He will be immediately apparent to everyone as a king[14].

Frankincense is used in incense, in Jewish rituals, and also in the Christian religion; it is used to show reverence for God. We cense icons and show reverence for the image of God that is within the saints, and we even cense any person because we recognize the image of God that is within them. After all, we are created in the image of God to obtain His likeness[15], and the Scripture says concerning us that we are gods![16] This means that we are to become like God and become perfect. This perfection is only possible because the perfect God, Jesus Christ, became man and made it possible for the perfection of human nature.

Myrrh was used in burial rituals. It is a desiccant and helps to keep the flesh from decaying. Of course, Christ’s flesh would never corrupt, but nonetheless, myrrh was anointed on His dead body by Joseph and Nicodemus.

The three gifts are prophecies. One states Jesus is a king, another that He is God, and another that He was born to die so that we could live.

On the right side of the icon is a shepherd blowing his horn. The apostle Matthew tells us that the shepherds taking care of their flocks in their fields were the first to find out about the birth of Christ. At the top of the icon are the Angels, who are with great joy announcing the birth of Christ in the heavens.

At the bottom left of the icon, Joseph appears; he is being tempted by the devil. The devil is trying to convince him that Mary became pregnant because of adultery, but Joseph was warned of this deception in a dream, and he remained the protector of the birthgiver of his God and Savior. Some modern (not Orthodox) depictions of Joseph show him as a young man, but this was not so: he was an 85-year-old widower (among his children were James, called in Scripture the “brother of the Lord,” and also Jude, an elder brother of James, who because of his great humility called himself the “brother of James,” and also Simeon and Salome, who was the mother of the apostle John and his brother James). Joseph was appointed to take care of Mary, with the appearance of marriage.

The bottom right of the icon shows midwives washing the infant Jesus Christ. It is entirely appropriate to clean up the skin of a newborn baby, and this act shows emphatically that Jesus Christ is fully man, just as the gift of myrrh states that He is God.

Priest Seraphim Holland

[1] "Theotokos" means "birth giver of God". It is one of our most important dogmatic names. If Mary had given birth only to Christ, that is only a man, there's nothing impressive whatsoever about that. Mary gave birth to a man who is also God and we emphasize this by saying that she is the birth giver of God. The name "Jesus Christ" indicates that we believe the God man him to be both God and man, because the word "Jesus" means "Savior", and "Christ" means “anointed” - God is the one who anoints with the Holy Spirit, and men are anointed. Since Jesus Christ was perfect man and perfect God, as a man He was perfectly anointed with the Holy Spirit.

[2] John 19:39-40 "And there came also Nicodemus, which at the first came to Jesus by night, and brought a mixture of myrrh and aloes, about an hundred-pound weight.  (40)  Then took they the body of Jesus, and wound it in linen clothes with the spices, as the manner of the Jews is to bury."
