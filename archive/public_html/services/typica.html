Notes:

The Typica is a service that has two purposes. There are some days of the year with the Typica is served before some Special Divine or Presanctified liturgies, in Great Lent or Holy Week, and some other times during the year. This typical service, below does not apply to this purpose.

Its major purpose is to serve as a substitute when Divine Liturgy cannot be served on the day it normally would be served, such as Sunday. It is the perfect service for lay people to use when they are not able to attend the Divine Liturgy.

Its format is basically that of a shortened Divine liturgy, taking out all the parts that cannot be served without a priest. All of the ectenias, which are the prayers that are said by the deacon or the priest and answered by the choir, such as “In peace let us pray to the Lord”, which the choir answers: “Lord have mercy”, are omitted. All of the parts of the liturgy that have to do with the consecration of the bread and the wine into the body and blood of Christ are also omitted. The small entrance, which occurs just before the reading of the Epistle and Gospel, and the Great Entrance, in which the bread and the wine, are brought from the table of preparation to the altar, are omitted. Basically, what is left is the sung hymns.

If a person knows how to sing the hymns, it is good to sing them. It does not matter if you do not know how to sing the hymns; in this case, just chant them. A person learns how to chant by listening to others chant. If you’re not sure and have not been able to listen to someone, then just read them, without embellishing anything. Whether we sing, or chant, or just read, we should concentrate on making our voice understandable, and not interjecting our own emotions and opinions into the text. This is a learned thing. Don’t be upset if you feel awkward at first. Nobody gets good at something without not being good at it first.

Every Divine Liturgy begins with the blessing by the priest, and then the great Ectenia which consists of multiple petitions which are answered by the choir, and then three “antiphons”.

The Typica will begin with a blessing, which is modified. When a layman says something in place of the priests blessing, he will usually say: “Through the prayers of our holy fathers, Lord Jesus Christ our God, have mercy on us. Amen.”

In place of the Ectenia the “Trisagion prayers” are then said.

The antiphons are “introduced” by a common sequence that is in many Orthodox services, which, however, is not in the Divine liturgy. It is “Lord have mercy”, repeated 12 times, then “Glory to the Father and to the Son and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto the ages of ages, amen”, and then the three lined hymn, “O come let us worship…”. After this, the antiphons are said exactly as they are in liturgy. In the Divine liturgy, after each antiphon there will be a “small litany”, which is omitted in the Typica. That means that the three antiphons are sung one right after the other.

The small entrance is omitted, but the entry hymn, which occurs at the very end of the Small Entrance in the Divine liturgy, is included in the Typica.

In the Divine liturgy, after this many Troparia and Kontakia are sung. In the Typica, at this point, only the appointed Troparia are sung.

This is the point in the Divine Liturgy where the Epistle and Gospel are read, with their accompanying hymns before and after. In the Typica all of these are included, but any place where the priest would say “Wisdom!” or “Let us attend” is omitted.

After the Scripture readings, in the Divine liturgy, there may be a sermon, and it would be appropriate to read one here as well, or at the end of the service. This is optional.

 The rest of the Divine liturgy is almost completely dedicated to the consecration of the gifts. All of these specific prayers are omitted.

Therefore, we immediately then say the “Symbol of Faith” (the Creed), and then sing the appointed Kontakia. To be perfectly honest with you, I am not sure why they’re not just included where they would occur in their regular place in the Divine liturgy, which would be combined with the Troparia. It would not be a crime to include them there, but the Typica calls for them to be included after the (Creed) is said.

We finish the Typica with Psalm 33, which is generally sung at the end of the Divine liturgy, often when the priest is consuming the holy gifts. Then we sing the Megalynarion, as sung at Divine liturgy, however in the Typica we sing it is the last hymn, and in the Divine liturgy it is sung immediately after the consecration is finished.

Third Antiphon

 

The Third antiphon is sung by singing a verse from the Beatitudes from Matthew’s gospel, and then adding a hymn that is specific to the day being celebrated. If there are two people, it’s very useful to have one person saying the verses from the Beatitudes, and another to sing the hymns. Alternatively, people know how to sing together everybody can sing it together. If you don’t sing, then you may chant this.

Note: Troparia are read between these verses, as appointed by the typikon.

The Troparion maybe chanted or read. Chanting is basically like “reading”, but it is a little bit melodic. You will sing with one or two notes. If this is difficult, just read the troparion. It is customary to try to read or chant without giving your own emotional interpretation. This takes a little practice.

In Thy kingdom remember us, O Lord, * when Thou comest in Thy kingdom.

 

Blessed are the poor in spirit, * for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.

[If there are 12 troparia, the first one should be sung (or chanted) now.]

Blessed are they that mourn, * for they shall be comforted.

 

Blessed are the meek, * for they shall inherit the earth.

[If there are 10 troparia, the first one should be chanted (read)now.]

Blessed are they that hunger and thirst after righteousness, * for they shall be filled.

 

Blessed are the merciful, * for they shall obtain mercy.

[If there are 8 troparia verses, the first one should be chanted (read)now.]

Blessed are the pure in heart, * for they shall see God.

 

Blessed are the peacemakers, * for they shall be called the sons of God.

Blessed are they which are persecuted for righteousness' sake, * for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.

 

Blessed are ye, when men shall revile you, and persecute you, * and shall say all manner of evil against you falsely, for my sake.

 

Rejoice, and be exceeding glad, * for great is your reward in the heavens.

 

Glory to the Father, * and to the Son, * and to the Holy Spirit.

[The second to the last troparion should be chanted (read)now.]

 

Both now and ever, * and unto the ages of ages. Amen.

[The last troparion should be chanted (read)now.]

 

Entry Hymn

 

O come let us worship and fall down before Christ, [Who rose from the dead,] * O Son of God, save us who sing to Thee: Alleluia, alleluia, alleluia.

 

*The hymn above, is called “O come let us worship”. Many Orthodox hymns are named by the first couple words. The only part of the hymn which changes is the part in brackets.

On Sundays it is always “Who rose from the dead”.

During the period from Pascha until Ascension, we also use: “Who rose from the dead”

Outside of the Pascha period, on weekdays we sing: “Who art wondrous in the saints”, unless it is a feast of the Theotokos, in which case we sing “Through the prayers of the Theotokos”.

If it is a Great Feast of The Lord (for instance, Nativity of the Lord, or Theophany, or Transfiguration, and others), we sing the appointed Entry Verse, or “Introit”. This will be completely different and replace “O come let us worship.”

This entry verse will be found in the specific materials for that feast. Most of the time this is in the “Menaion”, which contains all of the special parts that change for any commemoration throughout the year that has a fixed date during the year. For instance, the Nativity of the Lord is always on December 25 on the church calendar, which is January 7 of the civil calendar area all of the special changes for the services for that feast are in the Menaion on that date.

Psalm 33

 

Note: This may be sung, or simply read.

I will bless the Lord at all times, * His praise shall continually be in my mouth. *In the Lord shall my soul be praised; * let the meek hear and be glad.

O magnify the Lord with me, * and let us exalt His name together. * I sought the Lord, and He heard me, * and delivered me from all my tribulations.

Come unto Him, and be enlightened, * and your faces shall not be ashamed. * This poor man cried, and the Lord heard him, * and saved him out of all his tribulations.

The angel of the Lord will encamp round about them that fear Him, * and will deliver them. * O taste and see that the Lord is good; * blessed is the man that hopeth in Him.

O fear the Lord, all ye His saints; * for there is no want to them that fear Him. * Rich men have turned poor and gone hungry; * but they that seek the Lord shall not be deprived of any good thing.

Come ye children, hearken unto me; * I will teach you the fear of the Lord. * What man is there that desireth life, * who loveth to see good days?

 Keep thy tongue from evil, * and thy lips from speaking guile. * Turn away from evil, and do good; * seek peace, and pursue it.

The eyes of the Lord are upon the righteous, * and His ears are opened unto their supplication. * The face of the Lord is against them that do evil, * utterly to destroy the remembrance of them from the earth.

The righteous cried, and the Lord heard them, * and He delivered them out of all their tribulations. * The Lord is nigh unto them that are of a contrite heart, * and He will save the humble of spirit. *

Many are the tribulations of the righteous, * and the Lord shall deliver them out of them all. * The Lord keepeth all their bones, * not one of them shall be broken.

The death of sinners is evil, * and they that hate the righteous shall do wrong. * The Lord will redeem the souls of His servants, * and none of them will do wrong that hope in Him.
